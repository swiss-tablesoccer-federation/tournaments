## 1. Introduction

These regulations serve as the basis for handling disciplinary cases. A disciplinary case arises when disruptive and incorrect behaviour is observed to an extent that requires action.

## 2. Structure and scope

This section sets out the structure and scope on which the disciplinary procedure of the Swiss Tablesoccer Federation (STF) is based.

### 2.1 Structure

#### Reporting procedure

The reporting procedure describes the process for reporting incorrect behaviour that requires action. Every natural person — whether an STF member or not, whether a player, an official (referee and tournament management) or a spectator — has the right and the possibility to use the reporting procedure. The reporting procedure is dealt with in detail in section 4.

#### Sanction grid for the disciplinary procedure

Incorrect behaviour and rule violations are classified into four escalation levels to assess their severity.

The grid covers minor undesired behaviour up to serious offences that may be prosecuted under criminal law, and uses a card system to track the escalation levels. The table below provides a rough mapping between the behavioural requirements and the associated levels.

| Behavioural requirement | Escalation level |
|---|---|
| Everyday, but undesired and disruptive behaviour that has no immediate consequences. | **Warning** (verbal) |
| Clear boundary violation which is sanctioned but does not entail consequences beyond the event. | **Multiple warning** → 🟨 (yellow card) |
| Clear boundary violation that results in disqualification from the entire event. The incident is reported to the federation in writing, reviewed by an independent commission, and can therefore have far-reaching consequences for the person concerned. | 🟨🟨 → 🟥 |
| Behaviour that must typically be passed on to the authorities and is prosecuted under criminal law. | 🟥 (red card) |

Concrete examples of behavioural requirements, a detailed description of the escalation levels and the possible associated consequences, sanctions and penal provisions are given in section 3.

### 2.2 Scope

The scope of these regulations covers all licensed STF clubs, players, officials (referees and tournament management) as well as the federation itself. The parties named hereby commit to knowing and complying with the official STF rules, the rules of the Ethics Charter, and the rules of these regulations.

## 3. Sanction grid and penal provisions

Anyone who falls within the scope of these regulations and disregards the provisions of the official STF rules, the provisions of the Ethics Charter, or the provisions of these regulations concerning incorrect behaviour will be punished, provided that the relevant facts are established.

To assess incorrect behaviour, it is classified in a sanction grid with four escalation levels. The final and definitive assessment as to whether a measure or a penal provision is required is reserved for the STF officials and an independent decision-making body. The independent decision-making body is only involved if violations of level 3 or 4 occur.

The behavioural requirements and the associated measures are described below, but they are not a fixed quantity and merely serve as a rough classification of the violation. The following list is by no means exhaustive and will be continuously expanded in the future in order to build as comprehensive a database of misconduct and measures as possible.

### 3.1 Level 1

#### Behavioural requirement

Minor incorrect behaviour towards officials, opponents, teammates, spectators or the federation:

- Improper behaviour: breach of decency and morals
- Unsportsmanlike conduct
- Time delay
- Bending rods / table manipulation
- Influencing the referee
- …

#### Measure

Minor irregularities and disruptions are reprimanded verbally by an official. The official's task is to prevent things from reaching an extent that would entail Level 2 measures.

→ Issuance of a verbal warning

### 3.2 Level 2

#### Behavioural requirement

Moderately incorrect behaviour towards officials, opponents, teammates, spectators or the federation:

- Multiple boundary violations of Level 1
- Insulting behaviour: defamatory or abusive statements or gestures, or any kind of contemptuous acts
- Match fixing / arrangements about the course of play
- Racist, sexist or discriminatory impulses
- Verbal violence
- Mild threats
- …

#### Measure

Incorrect behaviour of Level 2 is sanctioned but does not entail consequences beyond the event. The official's task is to prevent any escalation to a higher extent (Level 3).

→ The current match is counted as lost and a yellow card is issued to the person concerned
→ No report to the federation

### 3.3 Level 3

#### Behavioural requirement

Serious incorrect behaviour towards officials, opponents, teammates, spectators or the federation:

- 2× boundary violation of Level 2
- Aggressive behaviour: actual physical assault or aggressive or threatening behaviour
- Severe racism, sexism or discrimination
- Bribery
- Theft
- Massive verbal violence
- Serious threats
- …

#### Measure

Incorrect behaviour of Level 3 results in immediate disqualification from the entire event. The official is obliged to report the incident to the federation in writing. The report is reviewed by an independent commission and can have far-reaching consequences for the person concerned:

→ Fine
→ Multi-event suspension
→ Withdrawal of the licence

### 3.4 Level 4

#### Behavioural requirement

Very serious incorrect behaviour towards officials, opponents, teammates, spectators or the federation, which must be passed on to the authorities:

- Massive physical violence
- Sexual violence
- General acts that are prosecuted under criminal law

#### Measure

The official, the opponents, the teammates or the spectators are obliged to inform the authorities.

## 4. Reporting procedure

The reporting procedure represents the process for reporting incorrect behaviour and is shown in the diagram below. It is divided into 4 stages. In the first two stages, one party identifies a concrete case or has a suspicion. In the next stage, this is reviewed by an official (referee and tournament management) or by an independent decision-making body of the federation, and finally a decision is taken and measures are initiated.

The sequence is therefore: **Case → Party → Assessment → Decision / Measure**.

```
Case            Party                  Assessment         Decision
─────           ────────               ───────────        ────────
                ┌─────────────┐
                │  Player     │── Assessment ──┐
              ┌─┤  or         │                │
              │ │  spectator  │                ▼
Concrete   ───┤ └─────────────┘           ┌──────────┐    ┌─────────────────┐
case /        │                           │ Reporting├───►│ Independent     │
suspicion     │                           │  office  │    │ decision-making │── Decision /
              │                           └──────────┘    │ body            │   sanction
              │ ┌─────────────┐                ▲          └─────────────────┘
              └─┤  Official   │── Assessment ──┤
                └─────────────┘    Level 3 + 4 │
                                                │
                                  Level 1 + 2 ──┘
```

*Figure: Reporting process for incorrect behaviour*

Depending on whether the party identifying the case is an STF official (organiser or referee) or an independent party (e.g. a player or spectator), there are two different reporting paths A and B.

**Reporting path A** describes the process for independent parties (e.g. players or spectators). At an official STF event, they can report the case or suspicion to an STF official (referee or tournament management) and hand it over. The latter is then responsible during the event for the further follow-up, assessment and/or reporting of the incident.

If no STF official (referee and tournament management) is present, or if such an official is, for example, themselves under suspicion, the independent party (e.g. player or spectator) can also report the incident directly to the federation by means of the reporting form. In principle, this path is only to be chosen for offences of Level 3 and 4. In the case of a Level 4 offence, the competent authorities must be involved.

**Reporting path B** describes the alternative in which the case/suspicion is identified and processed directly by an STF official.

## 5. Reporting form

The reporting form is used to report incorrect behaviour — of Levels 3 and 4 — to the independent decision-making body (<rechtspflege.stf@gmail.com>).

As explained in section 4, the form can be submitted by two different parties. On the one hand by an official (referee and tournament management) of the federation who has observed incorrect behaviour at an official event and who is required by these regulations to report it. On the other hand, incorrect behaviour can also be reported by independent persons, teams or clubs.

The reporting form can be downloaded from the STF website.
